Nwazet Pi Camera Box Assembly Instructions

The laser-cut parts of the camera box are protected by an adhesive white or brown paper which needs to be removed before assembly.

Bill of Materials

  • Raspberry Pi model A or B (not revision 1)
  • Raspberry Pi Camera
  • 7x black matte acrylic plates
  • 2x thin clear acrylic plates
  • 4x 1/4" black screws
  • 4x 3/8" black screws
  • 4x hex nuts
  • 2x 3/4" M/F hex standoffs
  • 2x 1/4" F/F hex standoffs
  • 4x rubber feet

Optional for wall mounting:

  • wall mount
  • 2x M5 screws
  • 2x M5 nuts

Optional:

  • magnetic lens

Note

Acrylic is a hard and stiff plastic which is sensitive to stress concentrations and shares a certain level of fragility with glass. Acrylic can scratch easily and should be cleaned with a mild solution of dish detergent and warm water. Never use cleaners unsuitable for acrylic. Never use a dry, abrasive cloth.

The matte side of the plates should be on the outside, and the glossy side on the inside.

Here are all the pieces from the bill of materials, after the acrylic pieces have had their protective paper peeled off.

Optional: if you are going to use a wall mount, we are going to start by attaching the bottom wall of the case to the wall mount. If you are not going to use a wall mount, you may skip this step and the following one.

Optional: Attach the bottom wall to the mount using the M5 screws and nuts. Make sure the plate borders and the mount are parallel. The matte side of the plate should be directed to the mount, and the shiny side should be up.

We are now going to prepare the bottom plate for the Raspberry Pi, using two of the short black screws and the F/F short standouts.

Secure the hex standoffs using the screws, into the two smaller holes that are located at the bottom-right and top-left of the plate. The standoffs should be on the shiny side of the plate.

If you are using a model A Raspberry Pi, select the corresponding left plate.

If you are using a model B Raspberry Pi, select the other left plate, which has holes for the Ethernet and USB ports.

Insert the left plate into the correct slots in the bottom plate, keeping it together with the Raspberry Pi itself, which should have its holes aligned with the standoffs that we already attached to the bottom plate. Attach the Pi in place with the M/F hex standoffs.

Add the front plate by inserting it into the bottom plate slots, then righting it around the stereo and video ports.

Add the right plate by inserting it into the bottom place slots, then righting it.

Add the back plate by inserting it into the bottom plate slots, then righting it in front of the HDMI port.

We are now going to attach the camera to the top plate. We'll need the camera, four 3/8" black screws, four nuts, and the two clear acrylic plates.

Start by fitting the camera into the transparent plate that has only one upside-down-L-shaped hole. The plate should have a tight fit around the camera lens and the components that are on the same side of the board. If it can't, verify that you have oriented the plate properly and try flipping it around.

Fit the second acrylic plate around the other side of the camera board. The camera's ribbon cable should go through the T-shaped hole. Make sure the plate fit properly around the cable and camera board components. If it can't, verify that you have oriented the plate properly and try flipping it around.

The camera sandwich that we just prepared is now ready to be attached to the back of the top plate.

Optional: There often are some particles of acrylic dust remaining from the laser engraving where the optional lens attachment goes. If you are going to use an optional magnetic lens with the camera box, you can remove that dust using a bit of adhesive tape.

Optional: if you are going to use a magnetic lens, peel off the protective paper from the lens' adhesive washer.

Optional: if using a magnetic lens, apply the lens' adhesive washer into the round engraving around the square hole in the top plate.

Place the "lens sandwich" behind the top plate. The Pi camera lens should fit right into the square hole. The matte side of the place should be on the opposite side from the camera sandwich.

While holding the cable as shown on the picture, insert each of the four screws into the top of the plate and through the two clear acrylic plates, then secure them in place using the nuts on top of the clear acrylic plates. The clear acrylic plates should bend a little under the pressure. Screw the nuts enough that they hold the camera firmly to the top black plate, but not to the point where excessive constraints are imposed on the clear plates.

Repeat for each of the four screws and nuts.

Once this is done, the camera should be securely attached, and should be almost exactly level with the surface of the top black plate.

Locate the camera connector on the Raspberry Pi, to the left of the HDMI port and to the right of the Ethernet and USB ports (if you have a model A Pi, there is no Ethernet, of course, just an empty space). Very gently pull up both ends of the top part of the connector. It should make a small clicking sound and should move a little around the connector, leaving enough space to easily insert the camera's ribbon cable.

Insert the camera's ribbon cable into the camera connector, making sure the contacts face the HDMI port. Holding the cable to the bottom of the connector, and making sure it remains properly aligned, push the top part of the connector back down.

The cable should now be securely attached to the Raspberry Pi.

Bring the top plate, with its attached camera, closer to the rest of the box, making sure the ribbon cable makes its way inside the box out of the way from the standoffs. One way of doing that is to have the cable run from the Pi's connector, over the HDMI port, around the standoff, then back in the direction of the USB and Ethernet, and then back to the camera. The important thing here is to make sure that the cable doesn't get stuck between the standoffs and the top plate, which would damage it.

Carefully close the box by gently pressing on the top plate, aligning its slots with the walls of the box.

Secure the box closed with the two remaining black screws.

Optional: if you have a magnetic lens, now is the time to attach it to the box. Look into the lens and move it around so that the Pi camera lens is at the exact center of the magnetic lens. This will minimize aberrations and blurry zones when shooting pictures with one of those lenses.

Optional: if you are using a wall mount, locate the small screw on top of the mount and remove it.

Optional: if you are using a wall mount, the screw should have liberated the metal part of the mount. This metal part is what you need to attach to your wall using the proper fasteners, depending on the type of wall. The provided fasteners may not be adequate, so please exercise caution, common sense and best practices, even if the load from the camera box is relatively light. The screw hole should face up. Once the metal plate is secured to the wall, you may insert the camera onto it, and secure it in place by putting the screw back. And... we're done!
